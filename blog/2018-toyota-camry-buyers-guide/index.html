Buying a 2018 Toyota Camry: the honest guide

By AutoVetting Editorial · Published May 31, 2026

The 8th-generation Camry (chassis code XV70) arrived in 2018 as Toyota's most significant overhaul of the nameplate in more than a decade. Sharper styling, a genuinely sporty chassis option (SE/XSE), and a completely reengineered powertrain lineup. The 2.5L A25A-FKS four-cylinder replaced the old 2AR-FE and the 3.5L 2GR-FKS V6 carried forward with refinements. This generation also launched Toyota's standard-equipment safety suite — Toyota Safety Sense-P — across all trims.

For a used-car buyer, the 2018 Camry is one of the stronger value plays in its segment: well-depreciated by now, low repair costs in the first 150k miles, and a parts ecosystem Toyota has been building for decades. But there are real things to check before you write a check.

TL;DR: The 2018 Toyota Camry (XV70) is one of the safest used buys under $25k, but your inspection hinges on the engine. The 2.5L A25A-FKS four-cylinder1 is the volume engine — it carries the Denso low-pressure fuel pump recall2 and a minor early-build oil-consumption pattern, both worth verifying. The 3.5L 2GR-FKS V6 is essentially a clean bill of health. The biggest named risk is the fuel pump recall (stall/no-start); confirm it's closed by VIN. The safest buy is a V6 XLE/XSE, or a 4-cylinder with the recall confirmed remedied and documented oil-consumption history.

The one thing most buyers miss

The 8th VIN digit tells you which engine the car has — and that changes your inspection priorities completely.

  • Position 8 = A → 2.5L A25A-FKS 4-cylinder. This is the car most buyers purchase. It carries active recall 20V-012 (Denso fuel pump — potential stall/no-start) and has documented oil consumption reports in a subset of early production. Verify both before you buy.
  • Position 8 = 6 → 3.5L 2GR-FKS V6. Essentially a clean bill of health. The V6 2GR family has been in production since 2005 without significant reliability concerns; the -FKS variant added direct injection without changing that track record.

Decode the VIN to confirm the engine before you show up at the lot.

Known issues

1. Oil consumption — A25A-FKS 4-cylinder

Toyota's A25A-FKS1 is a modern, thermally efficient engine, but a subset of owners report consuming oil at 1 quart per 3,000–5,000 miles — measurable, if not catastrophic. Toyota's own definition of "acceptable" oil consumption runs up to 1 quart per 1,200 miles (a standard they share with most manufacturers), but you should expect significantly better performance than that floor.

What to do: Pull the dipstick on inspection. Ask how frequently the seller tops up between oil changes. If service records exist, look for notes about adding oil outside of change intervals. A cold start with blue smoke from the exhaust tailpipe is a red flag requiring a more careful evaluation.

The A25A is not a flawed engine — but oil monitoring is a real part of ownership in ways that the old 2AR wasn't.

This pattern is documented in NHTSA owner complaints for the A25A-FKS engine group.

2. Denso fuel pump recall — 20V-012

NHTSA recall 20V-0122 (expanded by 20V-682) covers 2018–2020 Camry 4-cylinders equipped with defective Denso low-pressure fuel pumps. The pump can fail intermittently or completely, causing a stall while driving or a no-start condition. This is a free dealer repair — but many affected cars have not had it completed.

What to do: Before the test drive, run the VIN at NHTSA's recall lookup3. If the recall is open, negotiate the repair into the deal or schedule it yourself before purchase. A car with an open fuel pump recall is not a reason to walk away; it's a reason to confirm the fix.

3. Acoustic laminated windshield cost — XSE, XLE, TRD trims

Toyota equipped the XSE, XLE, and V6 variants with acoustic laminated glass for cabin noise reduction. This glass is not interchangeable with standard windshields — and a chip that would be a $250 patch on a base LE Camry becomes a $500–$1,100 full replacement on an XSE.

More importantly: the Pre-Collision System camera mounts behind the windshield. If a prior owner replaced the windshield with non-acoustic glass and skipped ADAS recalibration, the safety systems may be compromised or detuned.

What to do: Inspect the windshield for chips, cracks, and edge delamination. For XSE/XLE cars specifically, ask if any prior windshield work was done and whether dealer recalibration followed. A car with a clearly replaced windshield and no calibration records is worth a significant price deduction or a skip.

4. Entune 3.0 — no CarPlay out of the box

The 2018 Camry launched with Toyota's Entune 3.0 infotainment system. It did not ship with Apple CarPlay or Android Auto — Toyota added CarPlay support via a free software update at dealers roughly a year after launch.

This is a convenience issue, not a reliability issue. But buyers who expect CarPlay without checking should verify the update has been applied. It is a free fix at any Toyota dealer if not done.

5. Sunroof drain clogs (moonroof-equipped trims)

Toyota moonroofs have a documented drain tube clog pattern across multiple generations. When the drains block, water overflows behind the headliner and down the A-pillars — by the time visible staining appears, significant water intrusion has already occurred. The fix itself is simple (compressed air or a flexible wire through the drain ports), but finding it late is expensive.

What to do: On moonroof-equipped cars, check the headliner around the roof frame for any soft spots, discoloration, or sagging. Check the A-pillars for moisture or staining. A musty smell in the cabin with no obvious source is often drain-related.

Best year (within the XV70 generation)

The 2018 launch year is solid, with one caveat: the Denso fuel pump recall (20V-012) concentrated in 2018–2019 production. A 2020 or 2021 Camry with the same XV70 platform avoids that recall batch and adds minor refinements, but will cost $2,000–$4,000 more on the used market.

Within the 2018 model year: the XLE and XSE trims offer the best feature-to-cost ratio, but the added windshield replacement cost is a real ownership consideration. The LE or SE with acoustic glass adds a subtle long-term advantage: it takes the same chip patches as any standard windshield.

Avoid year

The XV70 generation runs cleanly through 2023. There is no "avoid year" within this generation in the way the old 2012–2014 XV50 had documented transmission issues. The A25A oil consumption pattern skews toward early production (2018–2019) but is not universal enough to condemn those years categorically.

If you are buying specifically to avoid oil consumption risk, a 2020+ with updated production tolerances is slightly preferable — though service records showing clean consumption history on a 2018 are equally reassuring.

What to pay

As of 2026, clean 2018 Camrys with 60,000–90,000 miles are trading in the $17,000–$21,000 range depending on trim. Specific guidance:

  • LE / SE (I4): $16,500–$19,000 for a clean example under 80k miles
  • XSE / XLE (I4): $19,000–$22,000
  • XSE V6 / XLE V6: $22,000–$25,000 — the V6 commands a meaningful premium because the 2GR-FKS is a significantly more capable engine
  • Hybrid LE/SE/XSE: $20,000–$24,000 — hybrid battery health is the key variable; verify with an OBD scan before committing

Price deductions to negotiate: open recall 20V-012 (~$200–$300 equivalent), windshield crack on acoustic-glass trims ($500–$800), no oil consumption documentation on high-mileage I4 examples (case-by-case). See where a given car lands against our editorial picks on the Camry's Pinpoint card.

Inspection priorities

Run the full inspection checklist at autovetting.com/inspect/?year=2018&make=Toyota&model=Camry before any purchase. The highest-value items for this specific vehicle:

Powertrain:

  • Pull the dipstick and check oil level. Ask about top-off frequency.
  • Cold start the engine and watch for blue smoke.
  • Check recall 20V-012 at NHTSA's recall lookup3 using the VIN.
  • On V6 models: cold-start and listen for timing chain rattle beyond 4 seconds.
  • On Hybrid: scan for battery warning lights and run a vPIC decode to confirm hybrid powertrain.

Body and glass:

  • Inspect windshield carefully on XSE/XLE/TRD trims — chip costs are 2–4x higher than base trims.
  • Ask if any windshield work was done; verify ADAS recalibration followed.
  • Check headliner and A-pillars for water intrusion on moonroof cars.

Electrical:

  • Confirm Toyota Safety Sense-P sensors are intact (forward camera behind mirror, front radar under bumper).
  • Test Radar Cruise Control on a clear highway stretch.
  • Check Entune CarPlay update status.

Verdict

The 2018 Toyota Camry XV70 is one of the most competent used-car buys in its segment and price range. It delivers near-luxury interior quality, a genuinely sporty chassis in SE/XSE trim, and Toyota's proven reliability record on a modern platform. The A25A oil consumption issue and the Denso fuel pump recall are real items to verify — but neither is remotely close to the structural concerns that make some competing mid-size sedans risky at equivalent mileage.

The full inspection checklist takes 20–30 minutes and covers all 18 inspection points for this vehicle. Decoding the VIN will confirm the engine variant (A25A vs. 2GR-FKS) and recall completion status before you leave your house. Do both.

Frequently asked questions

Is the 2018 Toyota Camry reliable?

Yes — the XV70 Camry is one of the safest used buys in its segment, with low repair costs through the first 150,000 miles. The two items to verify are the Denso fuel pump recall on 4-cylinder cars and a minor early-build oil-consumption pattern on the A25A engine. The 3.5L V6 is essentially a clean bill of health.

Which 2018 Camry engine is best?

The 3.5L 2GR-FKS V6 is the most trouble-free choice — the 2GR family has been in production since 2005 without significant reliability concerns. The 2.5L A25A-FKS four-cylinder is the volume engine and perfectly sound, but it carries the fuel pump recall and a subset of early cars consume some oil, so it needs a closer look.

What is recall 20V-012 on the Toyota Camry?

It's the Denso low-pressure fuel pump recall (expanded by 20V-682) covering 2018–2020 Camry 4-cylinders. The pump can fail and cause a stall while driving or a no-start condition. The fix is a free fuel-pump replacement at a Toyota dealer, so confirm it's been completed by VIN before you buy.

Does the 2018 Camry burn oil?

A subset of early-production A25A-FKS four-cylinders report consuming roughly a quart every 3,000–5,000 miles — measurable but not catastrophic. Pull the dipstick on inspection, ask how often the seller tops up, and watch for blue smoke on a cold start. The V6 does not share this pattern.

Does the 2018 Camry have Apple CarPlay?

Not out of the box — the 2018 Camry launched with Entune 3.0 without CarPlay or Android Auto. Toyota added CarPlay via a free dealer software update roughly a year after launch, so verify the update has been applied if phone integration matters to you.
