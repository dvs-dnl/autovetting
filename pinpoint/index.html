Acura TSX

2014 · FWD · 2.4L K24 I4 (201hp) or 3.5L V6 (280hp)

The strongest single pick. Premium feel, K24 reliability, sporty handling.

The TSX is built on the same K24 four-cylinder you'll find in a CR-V, the Acura RDX, and (until 2015) the Honda Accord. It's one of the most reliable Japanese engines of the last 30 years — owners regularly hit 250,000 miles with nothing more than oil, filters, and tires.

The 2014 was the last model year. Acura killed it to consolidate on the TLX, which means inventory is now low but the bugs were worked out. The interior is leather and sunroof standard; the optional Technology Package adds ELS audio that punches well above its segment.

For your budget, this is the one we'd buy. Not because it's the most exciting, but because nothing else at $7,500 gives you premium-segment feel with this kind of mechanical certainty.

Skip these

Walk-away cars in this band.

Listings you'll see at this price point that look fine on paper. They're not.

Chevy Bolt without battery replacement

Active fire-risk recall. Verify the replacement VIN at experience.gm.com before any purchase. Most cheap Bolts haven't had it done.

Early Nissan Leaf (2011–2014) in hot states

Air-cooled battery dies fast in Phoenix heat. May have under 40 miles of real-world range. The "Lizard" 2015 pack is the earliest survivable.

BMW 3 Series / Audi A4 / Infiniti G37

Cars hitting $7k are the ones nobody wants to fix. One repair eats the purchase price. Walk away unless you're a wrencher.

Toyota RAV4 EV (2012–2014)

Tesla powertrain in a Toyota body. Only 2,500 made. Drive-unit replacement from dealer: $17,467. Orphaned parts.

Range Rover / Land Rover (any pre-2016)

Air suspension, transfer case, electronics. Even the bargains become five-figure repairs. The British tax is real.

2012 Acura TSX

Before you buy

Five questions before you hand over money.

These are the things a $20 OBD scanner and a careful walk-around catch — before the test drive even happens. Take them to any car on your shortlist.

01 · Documentation
Are there service records — even partial?

Oil change history is the most revealing single document. Engines that went long between changes often show it after 150k. Even partial records are better than none.

02 · Electronics
Has anyone run an OBD scan?

A $20 scanner under the dash reads any stored codes — including ones cleared right before the sale. Ask the seller to allow a scan. If they won't, that's an answer.

03 · Structure
What does the undercarriage look like?

Rust hides where you can't see from the driver's seat — frame rails, rockers, wheel wells. In Phoenix it matters less. In the Midwest it's why cheap cars are cheap.

04 · History
Does the VIN show accidents or rollback?

Carfax and AutoCheck aren't perfect — private-sale accidents don't always show up. But a clean report rules out obvious red flags. Run one before the test drive.

05 · Verification
Will the seller allow a PPI?

$100–$150 at an independent shop is the single best money in used car buying. A mechanic on a lift sees what a test drive misses. Sellers who refuse are telling you something.

06 · EV / Hybrid
What's the battery state of health?

For EVs, the battery is the vehicle. State of health below 80% means meaningfully less range than the window sticker. Ask for a SoH report — dealers can pull one.
